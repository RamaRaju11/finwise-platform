Cash Flow = Money received this month − Money spent this month
Think of it like your personal bank account. If your paycheck is $5,000 and you spend $4,000, your cash flow is +$1,000. Simple as that — same idea for a business.
Why it matters more than profit: A business can look profitable on paper but still fail if cash doesn't come in at the right time. Example: you invoice $50,000 but the client pays in 60 days. Meanwhile, rent and salaries are due today. That's a cash flow crisis even with a "profitable" business.
Immediate actions (this month):
  • Identify the top 3 expense categories and cut 10-15% from each
  • Chase overdue invoices — unpaid receivables kill cash flow
  • Negotiate with suppliers for 30-day payment terms instead of immediate
  • Pause any non-essential subscriptions or services
Longer term:
  • Raise prices by 5-10% — most customers accept small increases
  • Add a recurring revenue stream (catering contracts, subscriptions)
  • Review staff scheduling — labour is usually the largest variable cost

🟢 Healthy P&L

  • Gross margin 60%+ for food service
  • Net profit margin above 10%
  • EBIT (operating profit) positive — core business works

🔴 Unhealthy P&L

  • Gross margin below 30% — COGS eating all revenue
  • Net loss (spending more than earning annually)
  • Negative EBIT — business model doesn't work before interest/tax

COGS (Cost of Goods Sold) = Direct cost to make/deliver what you sell. For a bakery: flour, butter, packaging. For a consultant: subcontractor fees.
Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS. This is what's left before you pay rent, staff, or anything else. A 70% gross margin means you keep 70 cents from every $1 sold before overhead.
EBIT (Earnings Before Interest & Tax) = Gross Profit − Operating Expenses. This shows if your core business operations make money, ignoring how you financed it and taxes.
Net Profit = EBIT − Interest − Tax. The actual money your business makes after everything. This is what you can reinvest or take as owner's pay.
Fix gross margin first (most impactful):
  • Renegotiate supplier contracts — even 5% reduction on COGS adds thousands
  • Review your menu/product mix — drop low-margin items, push high-margin ones
  • Reduce waste — in food businesses, waste can be 8-15% of COGS
Then fix operating expenses:
  • Rent is often the biggest fixed cost — negotiate or sublease unused space
  • Review software/service subscriptions annually
  • Optimize staff scheduling to match demand patterns

🟢 Healthy Situation

  • Revenue is 50%+ above break-even point
  • Large safety margin absorbs slow months
  • Even a 40% revenue drop still keeps you profitable
  • High contribution margin (60%+) = efficient cost structure

🔴 Danger Situation

  • Revenue barely covers break-even — no room for error
  • Negative contribution margin = losing money on every sale
  • Fixed costs are too high relative to revenue
  • One slow week can push the entire month into loss
Break-Even Point = The sales number where you make exactly $0 profit and $0 loss. Below it: you lose money. Above it: every dollar is profit.

Bakery example: Fixed costs = $15,000/month. You keep 70 cents from every $1 sold after ingredients. So you need $15,000 ÷ 0.70 = $21,429 in sales just to cover fixed costs. Sell $40,000 → profit of $13,001.
Contribution Margin = What % of each sale "contributes" to covering your fixed costs and profit. A 70% margin means for a $10 coffee and cake sale, $7 goes toward rent, staff, and profit. $3 goes to ingredients.
  • Raise prices by 5-10%: Most customers don't leave over small price increases. A 5% price rise on $40K revenue = $2,000 extra/month straight to profit
  • Cut low-margin items: Drop menu items where COGS exceeds 40%. Focus on high-margin items (beverages, prepared foods)
  • Reduce fixed costs: Sublease unused space, renegotiate rent, audit software subscriptions
  • Add revenue streams: Catering, online orders, baked goods wholesale to local offices

🔴 Danger Runway (like our bakery right now)

  • Staff morale drops when payroll feels uncertain
Cash Runway = How long your business can pay all its bills using only the money already in the bank — even if you earn zero revenue tomorrow.

Formula: Cash in Bank ÷ Monthly Expenses = Months of Runway
$45,000 ÷ $30,200 = 1.5 months
Why 6 months? In the US, the average small business faces a major disruption (equipment failure, key customer leaving, market slowdown) every 2-3 years. Processing a bank loan takes 4-8 weeks. An SBA loan can take 60-90 days. If you only have 1.5 months of cash and something goes wrong, you may close before any help arrives. 6 months gives you time to react, pivot, and seek help without desperation.
Main Street Bakery needs $136,200 more in reserves. Here's how:
  • Set aside 15% of monthly profit: $13,000 × 15% = $1,950/month. Reaches 6 months in 5.8 years — too slow alone
  • Apply for an SBA line of credit: A $100,000 line of credit you never draw on = instant runway safety net. Only pay interest if you use it
  • Holiday season surge: November-December bakery revenues often 40-60% higher. Save the surplus instead of spending it
  • Cut the largest discretionary cost by 20%: Reducing expenses by $1,500/month both lowers burn rate AND builds reserves faster

DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) = Your cash flow ÷ your loan payments.
DSCR of 4.06 means: for every $1 you owe the bank, you earn $4.06 in cash. That's very comfortable.
DSCR of 1.0 means: you earn exactly $1 for every $1 owed — zero buffer. One bad week = default.
Banks want 1.25+ minimum. SBA wants 1.35+.
DTI (Debt-to-Income Ratio) = Total monthly loan payments ÷ Monthly revenue × 100.
DTI of 8% means only 8 cents of every $1 you earn goes to loan repayments. That's very healthy.
DTI above 40% is a red flag for most lenders. Above 50% = almost certain rejection.
  • Reduce the loan amount: Ask for $100K instead of $150K — lower EMI improves DSCR immediately
  • Extend the tenure: 84-month (7 year) loan has lower EMI than 60-month — improves both DSCR and DTI
  • Pay off existing debt first: Eliminating a smaller existing loan raises DSCR significantly
  • Increase revenue 10-15% before applying: Higher cash flow = higher DSCR
  • Wait 3 months: Consistent positive cash flow history strengthens the bank's confidence

Safe EMI = The maximum monthly loan payment your cash flow can absorb while keeping a 30% safety buffer.

Formula: Safe EMI = (Net Cash Flow × 37.5%) where 37.5% ensures DSCR stays above 1.25

Main Street Bakery: Net Cash Flow = $13,000. Safe EMI = $13,000 × 37.5% = $4,875. The $150K loan needs only $3,080/month — well under the limit.
Why "safe" matters vs "what the bank will lend": Banks calculate affordability based on credit history and revenue. They don't always account for your specific expense mix, seasonality, or planned cost increases. Their limit and your safe limit are very different numbers. Always use your own calculation first.

A financial ratio is just two numbers divided by each other to create a simple comparison. Banks invented them so they can compare completely different-sized businesses using the same scale.

Example: DSCR of 2.12 means the same thing whether your business earns $40K/month or $4M/month. It tells the bank: "for every $1 I owe you, I earn $2.12." That's universally understandable.
The 4 ratios every US lender checks:
  • DSCR ≥ 1.25 — Can you cover debt from income? (1.25 = SBA minimum)
  • DTI ≤ 40% — Is debt a small fraction of income?
  • Current Ratio ≥ 1.5 — Can you pay short-term bills? (from Working Capital tool)
  • Gross Margin ≥ 20% — Is the core business model efficient?

Section 179 = A US tax rule that lets you deduct the full cost of business equipment in the year you buy it, instead of slowly over 5-7 years (depreciation). Buying a $12,000 oven? Deduct the full $12,000 this year. Saves you $3,000-4,000 in taxes immediately.
SEP-IRA = A retirement account for self-employed people and small business owners. You can contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income (up to $66,000). Every dollar you put in reduces your taxable income by the same amount. It's saving for retirement AND reducing taxes simultaneously.
Quarterly Estimated Tax = The IRS expects self-employed people and business owners to pay taxes 4 times per year (April, June, September, January) rather than all at once in April. If you miss these, you pay a penalty even if you pay in full by April.

Mistake 1: Taking too much
  • Drawing $9,800/month (everything left after expenses) leaves zero buffer
  • One bad month + emergency repair = missed loan payment + personal financial stress
  • Banks see owner draws exceeding 50% of net cash flow as a risk factor
Mistake 2: Taking too little
  • Many first-gen business owners pay themselves $0 or below minimum wage
  • This creates an unrealistic picture of profitability — the business "looks" profitable but only because you're not paying for your own labor
  • When you eventually hire someone to do your job, the profit disappears
The right approach: Pay yourself a market-rate salary as an expense first. Then take additional draws from profit. This gives you a realistic view of true business profitability.
